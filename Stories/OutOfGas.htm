“Official PILOT LOGBOOK” is written in gold letters across the face in a way that makes it look like a toy for an aspiring 10 year old.  But, it’s an artifact that is familiar to thousands of pilots across the country.

 

Mine is frayed around the edges, showing the grey cardboard beneath the once pristine black textured surface.  Its size also makes me think of a toy.  At just over 4” tall by 8” wide, and with only 36 sheets of paper within its cardboard covers, this little book looks more an object of play than a serious record.

 

For me, flying is play.  I decided to learn to fly on a whim; responding to a general invitation in the locker room after my karate class.  “Who wants to learn to fly?”  I don’t think I responded right away, but caught up with Randy in the parking lot.  A few minutes later and we had an appointment for my first lesson.  It was on my birthday in 1985.  I was 25 years old.

 

For the next 7 months, I worked hard at filling just the first 6 pages of that book.  By March 20, 1986, I had written in the little book 35 times.  I had flown a total of 39.1 hours; almost enough to satisfy the minimum flight requirement to receive my private pilot’s license.

 

Those that have attempted their flight training in the Seattle winter will probably appreciate how difficult it can be to string together the hours needed.  It’s very difficult to find good enough weather to allow a student pilot to venture out from the airport; especially if he will be flying alone, as I had been since December.  December 1st was the first time I got to make an entry in the “PIC Inc. Solo” column.  “Pilot in Command”.  After 22.8 hours, I was finally, and undeniably the only personal controlling that aircraft.

 

But it’s the 36th entry in this book that causes me to set words to paper.  It’s an entry of which I’m at once both proud and ashamed.  “Attempted long XC”.  Before a pilot can get his wings he must plan and make a long cross-country flight.  I had been waiting to take this flight for many weeks; waiting for a day where the weather was sufficiently good over the area of my flight that my instructor could feel comfortable that I would be safe to let fly away for many hours on my own.

 

 

To call me “analytical” is an understatement.  I work as a computer programmer, and have always prided myself on my reasoning abilities.  So I jumped into the planning process for this flight with enthusiasm.  I had calculated every part of the flight in detail.  I knew exactly how many minutes before I would have to make each turn.  And I knew precisely how much fuel I would use on each leg of the flight.

 

My plan was to fly from the Renton airport at the south end of Lake Washington and fly toward the coast at Hoquiam.  There, I would do a touch and go and fly on to Astoria, Oregon.  At Astoria, I would have my official “full stop” required by the regulations.  The FAA has spelled out in explicit detail what qualifies as a “cross country” flight.  Each person who applies for a private pilot certificate must complete:

 

§ 61.109 (5.ii) One solo cross-country flight of at least 150 nautical miles total distance, with full-stop landings at a minimum of three points, and one segment of the flight consisting of a straight-line distance of at least 50 nautical miles between takeoff and landing locations.

 

From Astoria, I would continue to Hillsboro, Oregon, where I would stop to refuel.  And, finally, I would return to Renton to complete my 300 nautical mile flight (double the required minimum).  A Cessna 150 holds 22.5 gallons of fuel, and consumes between 5 and 6 gallons per hour.  That gives you 3 hours and 45 minutes (3:45) of flying time.   But the FAA prudently requires that you should plan to land with no less than half and hour of fuel remaining – giving me an expected range of 3:15.  Flying at 90 nautical miles per hour (just over 100 mph), you should be able to cover 292 nautical miles before refueling, with fuel to spare.

 

The total distance from Renton to Hoquiam to Astoria to Hillsboro is 187 nautical miles.  So one would expect to be able to reach Hillsboro in just over 2 hours and refuel there (and in fact should be able to land with about 1:45 of fuel remaining).  That was my plan.  My big mistake was in believing it.

 

Before departing, Randy and I had a conference.  We reviewed the weather information I had obtained.  It was sunny in Seattle, and we had clear weather forecast for my entire route.  Some spots on the coast were reporting some gusty winds.  We decided I could go, and I told Randy I’d call him when I got back.

 

Taking off from Renton, it was a wonderful day.  The sun was shining, and while there were a few clouds, it was a very clear day.  Like anyone embarking on a flight alone like this for the first time, I was nervous; particularly about getting lost.  As a student pilot, I am required to navigate using “visual flight rules” – VFR.  That means that my primary means of navigation is to look down at the ground, recognize where I am, and proceed accordingly.

 

When you look at an aviation map – there are all kinds of useful landmarks.  Roads, neatly labeled cities, railroad tracks, towers, rivers, lakes, mountains, etc.  But when you get in the air – the ground looks very much the same every where you look.  Here in the northwest, it’s mostly trees, with the occasional hill or distant mountain.  You occasionally see roads, but of course they all look the same.

 

As a student pilot, you become very familiar with your “practice area” – the immediate vicinity of your home airport becomes populated with dozens of familiar and comforting land marks.  You learn to fly over this road to reach that water tower at which point you can turn toward the airport.

 

But on a long cross country flight, there are no familiar landmarks.  And so you cheat.  In 1986, there were basically two ways to cheat in a private aircraft.  First, you have a compass.  I know, for example, that if I take off from Renton, and fly on a compass heading of 228º for an hour and 3 minutes, I’m going to be (approximately) over the Hoquiam airport.

 

The second way to cheat is to use the VOR navigation system.  Each major airport broadcasts a radio signal from which you can determine your relative direction to or from that airport.  There are “highways in the sky” that use these radio signals.   In fact, there is one, with the romantic moniker “V-27” that takes you from Seattle to Hoquiam on a heading of 227º.

 

My instructor taught me to use every tool available to me, “cheating” or not.  So using compass, stopwatch, VOR, my map, and paper and pencil I picked my way to Hoquiam identifying as many landmarks as I could as I worked my way across the map.

 

But it was getting pretty windy.  Really windy. Occasionally my little 2-seat airplane would jump up 100 feet before I could bring it back down.  Or one wing would lift in the air, pitching the plane to the left or right.  I was starting to get uncomfortable; I was actually starting to get kind of air sick.

 

Air sickness was something that I had to really overcome during my training.  I was one of those kids that always had to sit with the window open on long car trips.  I’ve been very susceptible to motion sickness.  When I started to learn to fly, I had numerous bouts with it.  Once when Randy and I were approaching a landing at Renton, I got so sick I had to let Randy take over the controls.  I opened my window (yes, you can DO that on a small plane), and threw up over Lake Washington.  Unfortunately, the wind ripped my glasses off my face as well, so I got to watch them tumble away into the lake as Randy brought us down.

 

But it was something that got better over time.  I stuck with it, and as I got more accustomed to the movements of the plane (especially when I was controlling it), I had fewer incidents of air sickness.

 

I never got to the point where I had to throw up on this flight, but I did get to the edge.  I was no longer just really happy and excited to be making this flight.  I really wanted it to be over, more than anything.  I finally reached the Hoquiam airport where I attempted a touch and go.  As I got closer to the ground, the gusty winds did not let up.  The wind was blowing across the runway (from left to right) so that I had a very hard time even keeping the plane lined up with the runway.

 

I knew that I didn’t HAVE to touch down here – all that was required by the regulations were 3 full-stop landings.  I could get those at Astoria, Hillsboro, and finally back at Renton.  So I gave up my approach, and pointed the plane in the direction of Astoria.

 

The wind continued to blow fiercely, though a bit more steadily.   Luckily, Astoria is one of those airports with runways pointing in several directions, so I was able to land facing directly into the wind without a large cross wind blowing me off course.  Landing into a large headwind is almost like landing in a helicopter; you SLOWLY approach the runway getting closer and closer to the ground.

 

I was not very familiar with the Astoria airport.  I found a place to park, got out and went to the bathroom to splash some water on my face.  I’m was starting to feel better, but also felt like I’ve been through an ordeal.  It’s getting late though, as the wind has slowed me up considerably.  I glanced at my fuel guage.  It looked kind of low – but I also knew that the gauges can’t really be trusted to be totally accurate.  I glanced at a fuel truck down the field.  I could have goten fuel here – though that wasn’t my plan.  And besides, I knew from my preflight planning that I should have had plenty of fuel by the time I got to Hillsboro.

 

I decided to just get going.  It’s was only another 45 minutes to Hillsboro and I’d rather get fuel at an airport where I’m more familiar with the services.  Big mistake.  And I knew it almost immediately.

 

As I climbed back toward the east, I kept glancing at the fuel gauge.  Should I have gotten fuel after all?  By then I would have felt stupid taking off, only to return a few minutes later.  I felt committed to continue – though very nervous about how close I might be cutting it.

 

The next section of the flight was over the coastal mountains with some peaks reaching up to around 3,000 feet.  But there are practically no discernable landmarks – it’s mostly one indistinguishable hill after another, with the occasional uncharted logging road.  I’ve started to really think about conserving fuel at this point.  I didn’t want to have to climb any higher than I had to, as climbing consumes almost twice as much fuel as cruising straight and level.

 

Unfortunately, that meant that I soon lost reception of the VOR radio signal I’ve been using to navigate to Hillsboro.  Now all I had to go on was compass and stopwatch.  I started scanning desperately to identify the larger mountains and look for roads or transmission wires that are on the chart.

 

About ½ hour into this and I felt in a bad bind.  First, I didn’t really know how much fuel I had left, and second, I’m a bit lost.  I was about 10 miles south of the most optimal course to Hillsboro.

 

And then I got a shock.  The engine sputtered.  My first impulse was to look down and see what sort of emergency landing options I had.  I saw mostly trees – and a very rough and winding logging road.  And I was just barely above the ridge of a mountain off to my left.

 

I peered ahead of the plane.  I didn’t see ANYTHING that looked like civilization – just a lot of mountains ahead.  Then I looked to the left.  And I saw it.  Over the edge of the ridge I could see low and flat fields – farmland!  I immediately turned toward the ridge.  I had about 1000 feet of altitude above the ridge line and a mile to go to cross it.  The engine was cutting in and out.  Whenever I pointed the nose of the airplane up the engine quit.  When I pointed it down, it sputtered back on.

 

Milking the last few drops of fuel, I got the plane over the ridge line and out over the lower fields below.  But I was forced lower and lower by my failing engine.  About this time, I called “MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY” over the radio.  Portland radio picked me up and I explained that I’ve run out of fuel, and that I’m lost, but somewhere west of Hillsboro.

 

They couldn’t see me on radar – but they give me a LOT of work to do.  They wanted me to tell them my position, change radio frequencies (twice!), and change my transponder code.  In the end, it cost me a lot of time and they were not able to give me any direct assistance.

 

Getting within a few hundred feet of the ground, I now had to pick out a place to land.  I was flying above a valley floor which seemed to be filled with various types of farms.  Finally, I found a spot that looked like a perfect runway – a strip of ground that looked to be a road or path between two fields.  All I would have to do would be to turn right, align myself with the road, and set it down.

 

I made the turn, but I was already very low to the ground.  Barely after turning toward the road, my wheels contacted the ground.  I hadn’t had time to properly align with my makeshift runway, and so I veered off the left edge.

 

It was then that I thought that my airplane was breaking apart.  I was rolling across a field; it was a very bumpy ride.  And all about me I could see fragments of some broken debris flying up around me.  I later found out that I had been plowing across a field covered with surface irrigation pipes.  As I rolled by I was chopping the pipes up and that’s what I saw flying past my window.

 

I finally came to a stop.  I was alive.  And safe.  And uninjured.  I gave out a big whoop!  I tried then to contact Portland approach to tell them I had landed more or less safely.  But I was then too low to get good radio reception.

 

 

I left the plane to go find a telephone.  Poking my head into a nearby building I eventually found a very nice family that agreed to help me.  They drove me to Hillsboro (missing their weekly bowling night).  I suppose it was somewhat entertaining for them to have a strange young man drop out of the sky onto their farm.

 

I called the FAA, my instructor, and my wife.  The FAA asked me to stay in Hillsboro overnight so I could have a little chat with their accident prevention specialist.  So I got a hotel room, and had many hours to contemplate what had happened before I had to meet with the FAA the next morning.

 

The main lesson I came away with was to not place so much faith in up-front planning that you don’t reevaluate the situation as new information arrives.  Even though I knew I had a very ample margin of fuel when I took off – the new information of a very strong headwind should have signaled me to re-think that assumption.

 

Second, nobody is going to give me any brownie points for landing with the smallest amount of reserve fuel possible.  There is almost no reason not to carry as much fuel as practical for any flight.  There are many situations where you may need enough fuel not only to get your destination, but also then fly hundreds of miles to find a location where the weather is sufficient to land, or to account for unforeseen winds, or deviations due to weather.  More fuel means more options – it’s hard to have too much.

 

To paraphrase the common wisdom:

 

The three most useless things to a pilot are the altitude above you, runway behind you and the fuel you didn’t put in your tank.

 

I was amazingly lucky to come away with so little harm to me after making a mistake like this.  The FAA found it sufficient to just talk to me without giving me a warning, fine, or suspension.  My instructor and the plane owner flew down to help me recover the (undamaged) airplane from the field.  Even he farmer forgave me the damage I had caused to his field.  And of course, I was very thankful to walk away with injury (or death!).

 

That which does not kill me, can only make me stronger.

- Friedrich Wilhelm Nietzsche
