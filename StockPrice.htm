What should the price of a stock be?

How do you value a stock? On the most basic level, it’s what people will pay. But that circular argument gives no guidance. In today’s market, there are some stocks which have had such fantastic movements in price, that it appears completely irrational. One would like to be able to factor out the irrational and emotional factors behind a stock price and arrive at a more reasoned approach.

By the end of this article, I will give you the tools to build your own models of a company’s prospects for growth. And then you can set your own price for that stock based on your model, rather than blindly depending on the market to set a rational price. You will be able to tell if, and by how much, a stock is over (or under) valued. Alternatively, you will be able to plug in the current stock price for a stock, and understand under what scenarios that stock would actually be worth that amount.

Irrational Stock Pricing

The case of Amazon.com is one of the most striking today. When trading commenced (May 16, 1997), they opened at $3.46 per share (accounting for stock splits). Today (January 8th, 1999) they closed at $160.25. That’s represents a change in valuation of the company from $500 million to over $25 billion; a factor of 50 increase in 1.5 years.

And all the while, it defies the kind of fundamental analysis that would suggest a rational approach to attaining these values. Amazon has never earned a profit, for example. The barriers to entry for a competing product do not seem very high. They have entered into an established market (book sales) with powerful competitors.

The factors in favor of a high stock price is a new and rapidly expanding sales channel (the Internet), brand recognition, and their rapidly growing sales.

But how to balance these factors to arrive at a rational current stock price? At the moment, I get the impression that individual investors (the retail investor) is driving the price rise of the stock. Combined with the relatively small number of shares outstanding, everyone wants to be invested in companies that will participate in Internet commerce; Amazon is the premier buy, and so, people flock to the stock no matter what the price.

A rational model

I believe that people buy companies in order to realize a return on their investment. That’s why I buy a stock. So a believe in fundamental analysis, rather than technical analysis. Some people look at the pattern of the hills and valleys of a stocks price history, and try to predict the future. I’m embarrassed to note that in our day of the supremacy of rationalism over superstition that we still have people who subscribe to this mumbo-jumbo; technical analysis is the moral equivalent of reading tea leaves.

So, I ask myself, under what scenario could Amazon really be worth $160 per share? We have an excellent example of another stock which has also had phenomenal price growth over a relatively short time – Microsoft.

An historical example - Microsoft

Knowing what we know today, Microsoft was dramatically undervalued for most of the last decade. Not only has the stock price risen dramatically to today’s levels (which may or may not be justified), but also the earnings of the company have grown consistently and rapidly. If you believe a stock price is paying for future earnings, Microsoft has been an incredibly inexpensive stock for much of its history.

In order to understand what a prescient person would be paying for Microsoft stock, say 15 years ago, I took a look at Microsoft’s annual revenues, earnings, and stock price since 1985 (actually I only have earnings and revenue data for 1985 – the company went public in March of 1986; the first time a market price for the stock could be established).  Revenue and earnings are in millions of dollars. Microsoft is, obviously, a growing company. It has been able to ride the wave of the adoption of the personal computer into our everyday personal and business lives. It has also improved it’s efficiency in delivering those products, which reflect reduced costs. Each year, Microsoft has been able to build on the successes of the past, and make incremental improvements and gains.

When a company is able to do this, it creates a growth process. In fact, the growth in Microsoft’s earnings has been remarkably consistent: looking at the table above, you can see that Microsoft has been able to achieve between 20% and 85% year over year growth in earnings for the last 15 years (the net growth rate has been 49% per year over this 15 year period).  the following log scale chart shows the consistency of the growth rate over time:

An alternative way of modeling a change in earnings would be through a linear process. Under that model, a company adds a fixed amount of additional earnings each year. While, on paper that seems like a regular and orderly way to manage change in practice it is impractical. A company has to build it's resources and adapt over time. So do it's customers and suppliers. And it's ability to change is limited by it's current size. To grow linearly, it would have to perform fantasic feats of expansion in the early years (like increasing the workforce by a factor of 20 in a single year), and make realatively meager gains at the end of the expansion cycle (growing only a small percentage of the current workforce).

Any investment that produces value, and funnels that value back into the investment will produce a growth process. When comparing investments, we are really comparing growth rates. In order to put a fixed dollar pricing on a future income stream, you need to discount the fact that I could have invested that money in some other investment that would have given me a reasonable return.

This is expressed by the discount rate; the rate of return that one could reasonably expect to receive from an alternative investment strategy. A rational value for the discount rate is the long-term rate of return of the stock market or, more conservatively, the rate of return on government bonds. I typically use a value of about 10% to 12%.  Any future income flow needs to be disounted by the discount rate (compounded) for as many years in the future that it occurs.  For example, I would divide an income occuring 1 year from now by 110%.  One that occurs two years from now is divided by (110%)*(110%) = 121%.  In general an income occuring N years from now should be divided by (1+disc_rate)^N to determine the present value.

A very important fact to remember is that no growth process can proceed, unchecked, forever. There are resources constraints that limit the extent to which any entity can grow. For example, there are only so many humans capable of living on the planet, there are only so many hours in a day to work, etc. Every company will reach a maximum size (and then either decline or stabilize).

So, the crux of a company’s valuation is deciding to what size it will grow, and over what period of time. The more rapid the growth and the larger the size (of it’s earnings) the more valuable the stock.

Let’s assume that Microsoft’s earnings are going to be flat at 1997 levels forever. Under that model, we compare the theoretical value of the stock for each of the past 15 years, to it’s actual market price.  The theoretical stock value is equal to the present value of the future income flows.  So, by taking the actual income flows as well as the value of the future constant income stream, we can attribute a value to the stock for each of the years in the past.

Note that for the early years, Microsoft was trading at very low P/E ratios. The market was not expecting that Microsoft would be able to have the sustained 15 year growth curve that it has actually maintained. It is only in the most recent years that the market price reached or surpassed the theoretical price. Note that I have made the very pessimistic assumption that future earnings will be flat. Obviously the market price reflects an expectation of future growth.

What does the market expect of Amazon?

We have to decide what combination of growth rate and years of growth we will model for Amazon. I don’t think it reasonable to project out a very far number of years into the future. There are so many factors to consider, both technological and competitive, that any look beyond 10 to 20 years is completely speculative.

Let’s suppose a 10 year growth curve for Amazon, which will then level out to a constant earnings stream. Given Amazon’s current price, what growth rate must they achieve?

But wait a minute! Amazon doesn’t have any earnings today; they are losing money – and will continue to loose money for at least another year. So we really have to peer into their business a little deeper.

Today, Amazon sells books, music, and videos. But in the future they are going to expand into many more areas of commerce. Their model is to expand into markets where they can add value over a retail experience. That value has been to offer vast selection and services to help the customer find the products they want in that large pool of potential products.

Amazon does have current revenues - $250 million in the latest quarter; a 300% increase from the year before. Looking at their quarterly report to shareholders, their gross margin (the amount of money they make on each incremental sale of a book) is about 20%. In other words, they spend 80% of the price of a book on purchasing and shipping each book. If that were all their costs, they’d be making a profit. But they also have sales and marketing expenses to the extent that they are losing 20% on each sale.

Given Amazon’s position as a reseller, I would not expect them to be able to achieve margins equal to a company like Microsoft (20%-30% net) that has the ability to protect it’s ownership rights and make it very difficult for competitors to offer replacement products.

So, let’s assume that in the long run, Amazon will be able to achieve 10% net margins on their products.  So if we assume they could be making $100M per year at today's sales levels ($0.64 per share), what is the implied growth rate over the next 10 years to justify the current price of $160: 47%.  At the end of the 10 year period, Amazon will be earning $27.99 per share ($4.7 billion total earnings on sales of $47 billion).

Amazon is really in the business of selling information. And the information they sell is of a durable nature; it’s value does not rapidly decrease over time (unlike news and stock quotes). The total sales of the book, music, and video industry is now $XX billion per year. Supposing that Amazon is able to capture a large percentage of that market, say 30%, they could peak out at $XX billion per year. Their earnings could then approach $XX billion per year.

Plugging this most optimistic scenario into our model yields a rational stock price for Amazon at $XX per share.
